## What is Stoicism?

Stoicism is a school of Hellenistic philosophy founded by **Zeno of Citium** in Athens in the early 3rd century BC. It teaches the development of self-control and fortitude as a means of overcoming destructive emotions. The Stoics believed that becoming a clear and unbiased thinker allows one to understand the universal reason (*logos*).

The central teaching is the distinction between what is *up to us* (our judgements, impulses, desires, and aversions) and what is *not up to us* (body, reputation, property, external circumstances). Wisdom lies in focusing entirely on the former.

## Core Concepts

### The Dichotomy of Control

Epictetus opens the *Enchiridion* with: "Some things are in our control and others not." This single idea is the foundation of Stoic practice. You cannot control what happens to you — only how you respond.

### Virtue as the Highest Good

For the Stoics, virtue (*arete*) is the only true good. External things — wealth, health, reputation — are *indifferent* (*adiaphora*). They may be preferred or dispreferred, but they cannot make you happy or unhappy without your consent.

### The Four Virtues

1. **Wisdom** (*phronesis*) — knowing what is good, bad, and indifferent
2. **Justice** (*dikaiosyne*) — acting rightly toward others
3. **Courage** (*andreia*) — enduring hardship without complaint
4. **Temperance** (*sophrosyne*) — moderation in all things

### Living According to Nature

Human nature is rational and social. To live well is to exercise reason fully and to act for the benefit of the community. *Cosmopolitanism* — the idea that we are all citizens of the world — follows from this.

## The Three Stoic Teachers

### Epictetus (c. 50–135 AD)

Born a slave, Epictetus became one of the most influential Stoic teachers. His lectures, recorded by his student Arrian as the *Discourses* and condensed into the *Enchiridion*, are among the clearest expressions of Stoic practice.

> "Make the best use of what is in your power, and take the rest as it happens."

### Marcus Aurelius (121–180 AD)

Roman Emperor and philosopher, Marcus Aurelius wrote his *Meditations* as a private journal — reminders to himself to live as a Stoic. It remains one of the most widely read works of philosophy.

> "You have power over your mind, not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength."

### Seneca (c. 4 BC–65 AD)

Advisor to Emperor Nero, Seneca wrote extensively on Stoic ethics in letters and essays. His *Letters to Lucilius* explore death, time, friendship, and how to live well under pressure.

## Learning Path

A recommended progression through Stoic texts:

1. **Start** — *Enchiridion* (Epictetus) — short, direct, foundational
2. **Practice** — *Meditations* (Marcus Aurelius) — daily reflections, highly practical
3. **Letters** — *Letters to Lucilius* (Seneca) — 124 letters on ethics and living
4. **Deeper** — *Discourses* (Epictetus) — fuller lectures, more demanding
5. **Modern** — *A Guide to the Good Life* (Irvine) — contemporary application
